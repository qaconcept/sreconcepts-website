Cloud Suitability & Transformation Strategy Questionnaire

Target Audience: AWS / Cloud Senior Architects – Transition or Transformation Frameworks

Goal of this Questionnaire

To provide a data-driven, multi-dimensional assessment that determines:

  • Whether moving to cloud computing is beneficial for your organization
  • Which migration strategy (full cloud, hybrid, or on-premises retention) aligns with your business and technical constraints
  • The appropriate transition approach (lift & shift vs. refactoring vs. re-architecting)
  • Readiness gaps in cost, security, reliability, performance, people, sustainability, operations, data transfer, vendor lock‑in, and organizational change management

Summary

This questionnaire is structured into 17 domains reflecting the AWS Well-Architected Framework, real‑world cloud transformation lessons, and organizational change management mechanisms.

Each question uses a standard 1–5 scoring scale (1 = strongly disagree / very poor / completely unprepared; 5 = strongly agree / excellent / fully prepared). Red flag questions highlight structural or critical operational risks when scored ≤ 2.

After scoring, a comprehensive decision matrix, migration pattern reference tables, and an executive readiness dashboard help translate collected indicators into actionable board-ready recommendations.

Architect’s Note: This document serves as a structured pre‑qualification filter. If any red flag parameter is triggered within Security, Staff Ability, or Rollback domains, resolve those structural engineering vulnerabilities prior to investing capital into a full migration discovery phase.

Instructions for Respondents

  • Answer based strictly on your current state (not speculative future states or intended plans).
  • Use the 1–5 assessment scale transparently and honestly.
  • For each specified red flag item, note whether your internal score evaluates to ≤ 2 and mark the checkbox accordingly.
  • Gather quantitative inputs from finance, security, enterprise operations, and native application owners—not strictly infrastructure IT.

DOMAINS & QUESTIONS

Domain 1: Cost Optimization & Financial Readiness

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
1.1 We have a clear, itemized breakdown of our current on-premises total cost of ownership (TCO), including hardware lifecycle, software licenses, facility power/cooling, and staffing.
1.2 Our current infrastructure utilization (CPU, memory, storage) averages less than 40% for most servers, indicating idle capacity waste.
1.3 We have workloads with highly variable or seasonal demand (e.g., end-of-month spikes, holiday traffic) that make fixed capacity expensive.
1.4 Our finance team is comfortable shifting from CAPEX (hardware purchases) to OPEX (monthly cloud bills) and can adjust budgeting cycles accordingly.
1.5 We have the ability to implement cost governance (budget alerts, tagging, automated shutdown of non-production resources) without excessive overhead.
1.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: Our workloads run 24/7 with steady, predictable utilization and no significant variance – cloud may cost more than on-prem.

Domain 2: Security & Compliance

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
2.1 We understand the shared responsibility model (cloud provider secures the cloud, we secure things in the cloud) and have staff who can manage OS, application, and data security.
2.2 Our industry compliance requirements (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS, SOC, FedRAMP, etc.) have existing cloud provider attestations and regional data residency options.
2.3 We have the ability to implement identity and access management (IAM) with least privilege, multi-factor authentication (MFA), and centralized logging across cloud resources.
2.4 Our current security tooling (firewalls, IDS/IPS, SIEM) can integrate with cloud APIs or we are willing to adopt cloud-native equivalents (e.g., AWS GuardDuty, Security Hub).
2.5 We have no legal or contractual prohibitions against storing certain data types (e.g., state secrets, certain health records) on public cloud infrastructure.
2.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: We cannot achieve required encryption key management (bring your own key, HSM, or cloud KMS) or audit logging for compliance.

Domain 3: Reliability & Business Continuity

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
3.1 Our current on-premises environment has experienced more than 2 hours of unplanned downtime in the last 12 months due to hardware failure, power, or network issues.
3.2 Our recovery time objective (RTO) and recovery point objective (RPO) requirements can be met by cloud services (e.g., multi-AZ, backup, replication).
3.3 We have workloads that would benefit from geographically distributed, fault-tolerant architecture (e.g., active-active across regions).
3.4 Our organization can tolerate the inherent network latency of accessing cloud resources from our offices (especially if workloads are latency-sensitive).
3.5 We have a disaster recovery (DR) plan that currently requires a second physical site; cloud could replace or augment that site cost-effectively.
3.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: Our workloads require single-digit millisecond latency to on-premises industrial controllers, HPC with low-latency interconnects, or real-time trading systems – public cloud may not meet it.

Domain 4: Performance Efficiency

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
4.1 Our applications are designed (or can be adapted) to scale horizontally (adding more instances) rather than vertically (bigger servers).
4.2 We have workloads that require burstable or GPU/FPGA compute that is expensive or unavailable on-premises.
4.3 Our storage and database performance needs (IOPS, throughput) align with cloud service limits (e.g., EBS, S3, RDS).
4.4 We have performed load testing and know the peak resource requirements; we can leverage auto-scaling to meet demand without over-provisioning.
4.5 Our network bandwidth to a cloud region (measured via latency and throughput tests) is sufficient for data transfer and real-time sync.

Domain 5: Organizational & Staff Ability

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
5.1 Our IT staff have at least basic cloud knowledge (e.g., IaaS concepts, CLI/SDK, infrastructure as code) or are actively training.
5.2 We have (or can hire) a cloud architect or DevOps engineer who understands networking, security, and automation in cloud environments.
5.3 Our organizational culture embraces “you build it, you run it” and autonomous teams, rather than siloed ops and dev groups.
5.4 We have budget and time for upskilling (certifications, hands-on labs) and for potential temporary productivity dips during transition.
5.5 Our internal processes (change management, procurement, incident response) can adapt to cloud’s faster pace (e.g., automated deployments, ephemeral resources).
5.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: Our entire infrastructure is managed by a single, long-tenured admin with no documentation or automation – cloud migration would expose severe knowledge risk.

Domain 6: Sustainability (Environmental Impact)

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
6.1 Our organization has explicit sustainability goals (e.g., carbon reduction, renewable energy use) that cloud providers can help meet via their efficiency and renewables.
6.2 We currently run our own data center with poor power usage effectiveness (PUE > 1.8) and no access to renewable energy contracts.
6.3 We can redesign workloads to be “carbon-aware” (e.g., shifting batch jobs to regions/times with cleaner energy).
6.4 Our hardware refresh cycles are infrequent (≥5 years), leading to inefficient, power-hungry servers – cloud’s newer hardware reduces energy per compute unit.
6.5 We can schedule non-urgent batch jobs (ETL, backups, ML training) to run in cloud regions or time windows with lower carbon intensity.
6.6 We have measured our current on-premises carbon footprint and can compare against cloud provider’s publicly disclosed intensity (e.g., AWS carbon footprint tool).

Domain 7: Migration Strategy Indicators

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
7.1 Our applications are legacy, monolithic, poorly documented, and cannot be easily modified – making them candidates for “lift & shift” only.
7.2 We have the development capacity and testing resources to refactor or re-architect applications to use cloud-native services (e.g., serverless, managed databases, queues).
7.3 Our applications rely heavily on on-premises low-latency dependencies (e.g., manufacturing line, mainframe) that cannot move, suggesting a hybrid approach.
7.4 We have a clear mapping of data gravity (large datasets > 10 TB) and can evaluate whether moving compute to data (cloud) or data to compute (hybrid) is cheaper.
7.5 Our compliance or data residency laws require certain data to stay on-premises, forcing a hybrid or edge architecture.

Domain 8: Transition Resources & Timeline

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
8.1 We have identified a specific pilot workload (low risk, high business value) that can be migrated first to prove cloud value.
8.2 We have a dedicated migration budget covering discovery, tools (e.g., AWS Migration Evaluator, CloudEndure), partner consulting, and post-migration optimization.
8.3 We have allocated internal staff time (e.g., 20-50% of relevant engineers for 3-6 months) to execute the migration without halting daily operations.
8.4 We have established a Cloud Center of Excellence (CCoE) or at least a small cross-functional team with decision authority.
8.5 Our existing monitoring, backup, and CMDB tools can export data to cloud or we have a parallel discovery process.
8.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: We are under a critical regulatory or business deadline (e.g., data center lease ends in 3 months) with zero cloud experience – high risk of failure.

Domain 9: Hybrid vs. Full Migration & Refactoring Depth

(Qualitative Structural Evaluation – Answer post numerical assessment)

Domain 10: Application Portfolio & Dependency Mapping

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
10.1 We have an up-to-date application dependency map showing inter-app communication, database connections, message queues, and file shares.
10.2 We have identified “tiger teams” of applications with circular dependencies or hard-coded IP addresses that would break in a cloud environment.
10.3 We have classified each application by business criticality (tier 1-4) and can prioritize migration order accordingly.
10.4 We understand which applications share stateful data (e.g., same on-prem SAN volume) and cannot be migrated separately without refactoring.
10.5 We have tested network latency and bandwidth between our on-prem estate and target cloud region for each major application cluster.
10.6 Red Flag Risk Factor: Any mission-critical application has no documented owner or is running on undocumented, legacy hardware with no staging environment.

Domain 11: Data Transfer & Egress Costs

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
11.1 We have modeled the monthly data transfer volume from cloud to internet, cloud to on-prem (hybrid), and cloud to cloud/other regions.
11.2 Our workloads are not “data warehouse” or “analytics” jobs that regularly export terabytes to third-party tools (high egress risk).
11.3 We can use content delivery networks (CDN) or private interconnects (Direct Connect, ExpressRoute) to reduce egress costs for external users.
11.4 We have identified data sets that could remain on-prem with compute in cloud (e.g., using cloud VMs with on-prem storage via high-speed link) to avoid data movement costs.
11.5 Red Flag Risk Factor: Our users are globally distributed and we have no CDN; cloud egress would exceed compute savings.

Domain 12: Operational Model & Cloud FinOps

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
12.1 We have a designated Cloud FinOps role or team responsible for cost visibility, anomaly detection, and reserved instance/savings plan purchasing.
12.2 We can implement real-time budget alerts and automated shutdown policies for non-production environments (e.g., dev/test off-hours).
12.3 Our incident response and patch management processes can handle ephemeral, auto-scaling infrastructure (e.g., immutable images instead of patching running servers).
12.4 We have a tagging strategy (e.g., cost center, environment, application, owner) enforced at deployment time via policy-as-code.
12.5 We are prepared for cloud provider billing surprises (e.g., data transfer, API calls, NAT gateway, log ingestion) and have monitoring dashboards.

Domain 13: Vendor Lock-in & Exit Strategy

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
13.1 Our architecture avoids proprietary cloud services where open-source alternatives exist (e.g., Kubernetes instead of Fargate, PostgreSQL instead of DynamoDB) unless the lock-in is justified.
13.2 We have documented which cloud-native services (e.g., SQS, Lambda, CloudFront) would be costly or impossible to migrate away from.
13.3 We have a backup and restore process that can extract all data and configuration in a portable format (e.g., SQL dumps, Terraform state) at least quarterly.
13.4 Our contracts with the cloud provider include data portability rights and reasonable egress fee caps for exit scenarios.
13.5 Red Flag Risk Factor: We are migrating a 10-year-old Oracle database to RDS Oracle with no plan for future portability – vendor lock-in is severe.

Domain 14: Compliance & Audit Evidence Collection

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
14.1 We can generate automated, timestamped compliance evidence (e.g., CloudTrail logs, Config rules, VPC flow logs) for auditors without manual screenshots.
14.2 Our cloud environment can be logically isolated (e.g., separate AWS account or Azure subscription per compliance boundary) to simplify audits.
14.3 We have validated that our cloud provider’s region selection (e.g., AWS GovCloud, Azure Germany) matches our data sovereignty laws.
14.4 We have a plan for long-term log retention (1–7+ years) that includes tamper-proof storage (e.g., Object Lock, immutable buckets).

Domain 15: Migration Testing & Rollback Plan

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
15.1 We have a staging environment identical to production to test the migrated workload before cutover.
15.2 We have a documented, rehearsed rollback plan for each migration wave, with a maximum tolerated cutover time (e.g., 4-hour rollback window).
15.3 We can run both on-prem and cloud environments in parallel (dual-run) for a period to validate data consistency and performance.
15.4 Our CI/CD pipeline supports blue/green or canary deployments to the cloud target.
15.5 Red Flag Risk Factor: We have no ability to roll back to on-prem after migration due to hardware decommissioning or lease expiry without a buffer period.

Domain 16: Hybrid / Multi-Cloud Operations Complexity

(Conditional Module: Mandatory only if indicators from Domain 7.3 or 7.5 reflect scores ≥ 4)

# Question Baseline Requirement Score (1-5)
16.1 We have a unified identity provider (e.g., Azure AD, Okta) that works across on-prem and cloud.
16.2 We can manage networking (VPN, Direct Connect, routing, firewalls) that spans on-prem and multiple cloud VPCs without performance degradation.
16.3 Our monitoring and alerting system (e.g., Datadog, Prometheus, CloudWatch) aggregates metrics from both environments seamlessly.
16.4 We have budget for cross-cloud data transfer if we use multiple cloud providers for different workloads.

Step 3: Overall Cloud Suitability Guidance Matrix

Condition Formula Strategic Transformation Recommendation
No domain below 2.5 AND at least three domains above 4.0 🟢 Proceed with Full Cloud Migration (All workloads not explicitly latency/compliance constrained).
One or two domains below 2.5 BUT they are remediable 🟡 Phased Migration Execution – Start immediately with pilot asset class, build dedicated mitigation sprint loops for gap spaces.

Step 4: Migration Strategy Recommendation Routing Map

Organizational & Technical Profile Archetype Recommended Migration Pattern
Low organizational change ability (5.1–5.5 avg < 2.5) + legacy apps (7.1 > 4) Lift & shift (rehost) to cloud IaaS, then decouple or optimize post-migration.
Medium change ability + urgent need to minimize day-2 operations overhead Re-platform – Swap self-managed data structures/queues for cloud-native managed entities (AWS RDS, ElastiCache).
High change change capacity + modernized application decoupling footprints Refactor / Re-architect – Deploy event-driven paradigms, edge serverless computing, and decoupled containers.
Compliance or data gravity parameters pinning state on-site (7.3 or 7.5 > 4) Hybrid Migration – Compute/Stateless engines burst to cloud infrastructure; system of record/data layers remain anchored locally.
Ultra low-latency industrial execution dependencies (3.6 Red Flag Triggered) Hybrid Edge Deployment – Deploy AWS Outposts, Wavelength, or maintain structural bare-metal isolation.

Migration Pattern by Application Archetype (Qualitative Reference)

Application Archetype Recommended Pattern Engineering Core Rationale
Legacy monolith with no internal source modifications accessible Lift & Shift (Rehost) Safest structural approach; guarantees fast isolation from exiting facility timelines.
Custom software with modular components and baseline cloud engineering fluency Re-platform Drops system administrative operational drag without demanding a comprehensive codebase rewrite.
Stateless microservice-oriented nodes with active deployment loops Refactor to K8s / Serverless Maximizes raw utility hooks, elastic scale properties, and accurate operational efficiency goals.
Legacy Mainframe topologies / AS-400 engines Do NOT Migrate High catastrophic failure profiles; introduce translation abstraction layers or maintain localization.
Commercial Off-The-Shelf applications (COTS: SAP, Oracle EBS) Vendor Certification Route Strictly adhere to dedicated provider platforms (e.g., SAP on AWS) to avoid breaching software agreements.
